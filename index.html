01 / The emotional weight

Not one survivor
escaped poor sleep

Every single participant (N=8) reported sleep disturbance after their cancer experience. Anxiety came close, affecting 7 of 8. Depression and social isolation appeared in fewer cases but clustered in the same individuals, compounding their burden.

02 / The timeline of need

Support must travel
with the survivor

Mental health monitoring isn't a single event, it spans three distinct phases of the care arc: pre-surgery, early survivorship, and long-term follow-up. Most survivors needed support well after treatment ended.

Long-term follow-up was the most-needed phase by far: 6 of 8.

"The care doesn't stop at the clinic door. Neither should the monitoring."

03 / Technology tension

Open to wearables —
but who is left behind?

Six of eight survivors were enthusiastic about using wearables to track mental health. The two who were uncertain raised a quiet but critical concern: older adults may be systematically excluded by technology-first solutions.

"Tech enthusiasm can mask a digital equity problem, the demographic most affected by cancer is often the least comfortable with new technology."

04 / Trust & access

Nearly all said yes —
on their own terms

Support for digital monitoring was near universal at 7 of 8. But the conditions mattered deeply about who holds the data and whether patients control will determine whether the tool is adopted or avoided in real clinical settings.

The cancer team was the near-universal access preference, however a common idea was to train the team discussing mental health to promote sensitivity.

Key findings

Three principles survivors taught us

Monitor across the full arc
Long-term follow-up was the most needed phase. Mental health tools must travel with the patient, not stop at discharge.
Design for non-techies
Older survivors: the demographic most affected by cancer. Digital equity is a clinical issue.
Trust is earned, not assumed
Near-universal support came with conditions. Who sees the data and how the data is communicated and preserved matters.
